<h2>The Real Threat Isn't to Adult Websites - It's to the Open Internet Itself</h2><p>Forget what this law says on the label. Utah's <strong>Senate Bill 73</strong>, which takes effect on <strong>May 6, 2026</strong>, is framed as a child safety measure. In practice, it is the United States' first legislative attempt to dismantle a foundational privacy tool - the Virtual Private Network - by making websites legally responsible for the physical location of every user on Earth, including those deliberately hiding it.</p><p>That is not a minor regulatory tweak. It is a direct challenge to <strong>data sovereignty</strong>, the principle that the internet's architecture - built on anonymized routing, distributed trust, and jurisdictional ambiguity - should remain outside the reach of any single state legislature.</p><h2>What Senate Bill 73 Actually Does</h2><p>Formally titled the <em>Online Age Verification Amendments</em> and signed by <strong>Governor Spencer Cox</strong> on March 19, 2026, SB 73 works through two blunt mechanisms.</p><p><strong>First:</strong> Any person physically located in Utah is considered a Utah user, regardless of whether they are routing their traffic through a VPN, proxy server, or any other obfuscation layer.</p><p><strong>Second:</strong> Commercial websites that host a substantial portion of material harmful to minors are now prohibited from providing instructions on how to use a VPN to bypass age verification.</p><p>The <strong>Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF)</strong> flagged an immediate First Amendment problem with that second clause and described the enforcement model as a don't ask, don't tell style of enforcement.</p><h2>Winners and Losers: Who Benefits, Who Gets Hurt</h2><div As location-based enforcement expands, global internet services face rising infrastructure and legal complexity.</p><h2>The Technical Impossibility at the Heart of SB 73</h2><p>Blocking all VPN and proxy traffic in real time is a moving target. IP pools rotate continuously, and comprehensive universal blocklists do not exist. The practical response is usually over-compliance: broader checks, stricter identity gates, and higher friction for legitimate users.</p><h2>What Comes Next: Legislative Domino Effect</h2><p>Utah is not isolated.
